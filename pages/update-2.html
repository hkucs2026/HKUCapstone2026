5 judge dimensions, 1–5 Likert (click for details)?Why our rubric, not RAGAS?
RAGAS: Es et al. (2024), "RAGAS: Automated Evaluation of Retrieval Augmented Generation" · arXiv:2309.15217
  • Faithfulness — demoted: RAGAS penalizes answers with claims unsupported by retrieved context. Our deployed app is allowed to draw on parametric knowledge or web search when the corpus is silent (Modes B/C/D/W); those answers can still be correct and useful. We report Faithfulness as a citability/traceability signal only — not a quality gate. Correctness + Completeness + Safety carry the quality weight instead.
  • Completeness — newly added: RAGAS does not surface coverage as a separate dimension (its closest, Answer Correctness, conflates accuracy with reference similarity). Catches cherry-picked / truncated answers that pass faithfulness but skip steps a user actually needs.
  • Safety — domain-specific: deterministic keyword detection (electrical / gas / structural / asbestos / heights / main-plumbing) + LLM judge in one prompt. RAGAS's AspectCritic equivalent would be an extra LLM call per topic.
  • Cost: single judge call scores all 5 dimensions; RAGAS calls one LLM per metric (≈5× the cost).

Are these findings statistically defensible??How significance was tested
Each test pairs (case, generator) tuples across modes — same case, same generator, only the mode varies — and runs a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test. p < 0.05 means the difference is unlikely (<5%) to be noise. Wilcoxon (not t-test) is used because 1–5 Likert scores aren't normally distributed.
  • Confirmed   Agentic routing (Mode C) is genuinely better than deterministic fallback (Mode B). The +0.37 correctness gap is real, not noise.
  • Confirmed   Mode C wins on the corpus's home turf (queries the corpus is built to answer). Mode C scores 3.87 vs Mode D's 3.56 here — the gap is real.
  • Confirmed   Frontier-LLM training knowledge beats this corpus on average. Mode D's 0.32 lead over Mode A is real — the empirical inversion finding holds.
  • Tied at the mean   Mode C and Mode D have indistinguishable overall correctness. The aggregate "tie" masks two opposing significant effects (C wins on retrieve_and_answer, D wins on web_fallback); the choice between them depends on which case-type dominates real traffic plus iOS UX needs (Mode C returns sources; Mode D doesn't).
  • Counter-evidence   On long-tail queries the corpus doesn't cover, Mode D beats Mode C. Frontier-LLM training knowledge outperforms thin web snippets here. We tested adding openrouter:web_fetch to mitigate; the naive integration backfired (correctness dropped 0.32 overall, 0.62 on web_fallback) — consistent with the retrieval-distraction literature. Tighter tuning (lower max_uses, conditional invocation) is required before adoption.

Test Data & Limitations

Golden dataset structure, sample cases, and honest caveats
Caveats acknowledged up front
  • Reference answers are LLM-authored (Claude during dataset creation), not curated by a home-repair professional. Correctness is therefore a model-against-model-reference signal, not an expert-graded one.
  • 22 unique queries replicated across 5 modes × 8 LLMs = 876 cells. Single-pass per cell, no bootstrapped confidence intervals. Paired Wilcoxon tests on the headline contrasts are reported in "Are these findings statistically defensible?" above.
  • Text-only sweep. The multimodal pipeline is verified end-to-end via the smoke test (below), but those 6 image cases are not judge-scored — they are smoke tests, not formal eval cells.

Mode C × Kimi K2.6 answer (severity=low, cost=$0.044, fallback=web used):
What's likely happening Water pooling around the base of your kitchen faucet usually means the rubber O-rings that seal the spout to the faucet body have worn out, cracked, or become coated with mineral buildup. On single-handle faucets with a swivel spout, this is by far the most common cause. Alternatively, the mounting nut underneath the sink may have loosened, or the plumber's putty/silicone seal between the faucet base and the sink surface has failed. How serious is it Low — This is a nuisance leak that wastes water and can damage your countertop or cabinet over time, but it poses no immediate safety hazard. Difficulty Level Easy Estimated Total Time ~30–45 min What to try first 1. Confirm it's a base leak: Dry the faucet and countertop completely, lay a paper towel around the base, turn on the water, and watch for 30 seconds. If water appears where the faucet body meets the sink, it's a base leak. 2. Check if the leak appears only when swiveling the spout: If yes, the O-rings on the spout body are almost certainly worn. 3. Wiggle the faucet from above: If it feels loose, the mounting nut underneath may just need tightening. Tools you might need - Adjustable wrench or basin wrench - Allen wrench set (for handle screw) - Flathead screwdriver or utility knife - Replacement O-rings (match size, or buy an assortment kit ~$3–5) - Silicone-based plumber's grease - Plumber's putty or silicone caulk (if resealing base) - Rag/towel - Flashlight - Safety glasses Step-by-step fix 1. Shut off the water supply (~2min) Turn both hot and cold shut-off valves under the sink clockwise until snug. Open the faucet to drain remaining water and release pressure. Cover the sink drain with a rag so small parts don't fall in. [Source: web - https://theplumbingdirectory.com/how-to/fix-a-leaky-faucet-base] 2. Remove the faucet handle (~3min) Pry off the decorative cap on the handle with a flathead screwdriver. Loosen the hex screw underneath with an Allen wrench, then pull the handle straight off. 3. Remove the spout collar/dome (~3min) Unscrew or pull off the collar (the round dome-like piece) that holds the spout in place. If it's threaded, grip it with needle-nose pliers wrapped in a towel to avoid scratches. 4. Lift off the spout and inspect the O-rings (~3min) Pull the spout straight up—it may need a slight twist or wiggle. You'll see 2–3 rubber O-rings around the cylindrical spout body. Check for cracks, flattening, or mineral buildup. [Source: web - https://sinkengineering.com/kitchen-faucet-leaking-from-the-base/] 5. Replace the O-rings (~5min) Cut or roll off the old O-rings. Bring them to the hardware store to match diameter and thickness, or use an assortment kit. Coat the new O-rings generously with silicone plumber's grease, then slide them into place. Tip: Loop a piece of string over the O-ring to help expand it as you push it over the cartridge, then remove the string once it's seated in the groove. [Source: web - https://sinkengineering.com/kitchen-faucet-leaking-from-the-base/] 6. Clean and reassemble the spout (~5min) Wipe out the inside of the spout and the seal at the top of the neck. Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease. Push the spout back down over the new O-rings—it will take some firm pressure. Reattach the collar/dome and handle. 7. Check and tighten the mounting nut from below (~5min) With a basin wrench, tighten the large mounting nut(s) under the sink that hold the faucet body to the countertop. If the faucet still feels loose or leaks, you may need to remove it and reseal the base. 8. Reseal the faucet base if needed (~10min) If the putty or gasket seal has failed, disconnect supply lines, remove the mounting nuts, lift the faucet out, and clean all old putty/gasket material. Apply a fresh rope of plumber's putty (for metal/porcelain sinks) or silicone caulk (for granite/stone) under the base plate. Reinstall, tighten until putty squeezes out evenly, and wipe excess. [Source: web - https://helloportable.com/kitchen-faucet-leaking-at-base/] 9. Turn water back on and test (~2min) Slowly open the shut-off valves. Run the faucet and rotate the spout through its full range. Lay a dry paper towel around the base for 30 minutes—if it stays dry, you're done. When to call a professional - The faucet body itself is cracked or corroded (cannot be repaired, needs replacement). - The leak persists after replacing O-rings and resealing the base. - You cannot reach the mounting nuts due to a cramped cabinet layout. - You're uncomfortable working in tight spaces or worried about damaging the finish.

Mode C × Kimi K2.6 answer (severity=medium, cost=$0.0397, fallback=web used):
What's likely happening Your Whirlpool washer is displaying the F5 E2 error code, which indicates a door or lid lock failure. The washer's control board cannot verify that the door/lid is properly closed and locked, so it stops the cycle as a safety measure to prevent water spillage or injury during high-speed spin [Source: web - https://producthelp.whirlpool.com/Laundry/Washers/Top_Load_Washer/Error_Codes_or_Flashing_Lights/%22F%22_Codes/F5E2_-_Error_Code]. This is most commonly caused by an obstruction preventing proper closure, a worn door boot seal (on front-loaders), or a faulty door/lid lock assembly. How serious is it Medium — The washer won't operate until this is resolved, but the fix is usually straightforward. Always unplug the washer before any internal inspection or repair to avoid electric shock. Difficulty Level Easy to Medium (depending on whether simple clearing or part replacement is needed) Estimated Total Time ~15–45 min for basic troubleshooting; ~45–90 min if replacing the lock assembly or door boot What to try first 1. Open and firmly re-close the door/lid — listen for a definite click indicating the latch engaged. 2. Check for obstructions — look for clothing, socks, or debris caught in the door seal or lid lock area. 3. Power reset — unplug the washer for a full 5 minutes, then plug back in and try again [Source: web - https://producthelp.whirlpool.com/Laundry/Washers/Top_Load_Washer/Error_Codes_or_Flashing_Lights/%22F%22_Codes/F5E2_-_Error_Code]. Tools you might need - Phillips #2 screwdriver - Torx T20 screwdriver (for some models) - Multimeter (if testing the lock electrically) - Work gloves - Flashlight Step-by-step fix 1. Inspect for obstructions (~3min) Open the washer door/lid and check the rubber seal (front-load) or lid lock area (top-load) for any trapped clothing, socks, or debris. Remove anything you find. 2. Check door/lid closure (~2min) Close the door/lid firmly and listen for a solid click. On front-loaders, the door must be pushed until you hear a definite latch engagement. On top-loaders, ensure the lid closes completely flat without gaps. 3. Power reset the control board (~7min) Unplug the washer from the wall outlet (do not just turn off the power button). Wait a full 5 minutes to allow the control board to fully reset. Plug back in, wait 30 seconds, then try starting a cycle. 4. Inspect the door boot seal — front-load only (~5min) Run your hand around the rubber door boot feeling for tears, gaps, or spots where it has pulled away from the housing. A shifted or torn boot can prevent the door from closing fully [Source: web - https://errbase.com/whirlpool/washer/whirlpool-washer-f5-e2-error-code]. 5. Inspect the door/lid strike and hinges (~5min) Check the metal strike plate (the part that enters the lock) for bending or damage. Ensure the door/lid hinges aren't worn or misaligned, which can prevent proper closure. 6. Test the door/lid lock with a multimeter — if comfortable (~10min) Unplug the washer first. Remove the front or top panel to access the lock assembly. Set your multimeter to measure resistance (Ohms). With the door unlocked, the lock should read very low resistance (0–5 Ω). With the door closed and latched, resistance should increase substantially or read infinite/OL. If readings are off, the lock assembly is faulty [Source: web - https://www.appliancepartspros.com/c/troubleshooting-steps-to-resolve-an-f5-e2-error-on-a-whirlpool-washer/]. 7. Replace the faulty lock assembly if needed (~30–60min) Order a replacement door lock (front-load, ~$20–$40) or lid lock (top-load, ~$25–$60) specific to your model. Disconnect the electrical connector, remove mounting screws, install the new lock, and reconnect wiring. Reassemble the panel and test. When to call a professional - If the lock tests good but the F5 E2 error persists (possible control board issue) - If the door/lid frame is warped or damaged - If you see burnt, discolored, or corroded areas on the control board - If wiring harness shows burns or damage - If the washer is under warranty - If you're uncomfortable working with electrical components

What's likely happening When your toilet keeps running after flushing, water is either leaking from the tank into the bowl (usually because the rubber flapper isn't sealing properly) or the fill valve isn't shutting off when the tank is full. These are the two most common culprits inside the tank, and both are usually easy and inexpensive to fix. How serious is it Low — A running toilet wastes water and raises your bill, but it poses no immediate safety risk to your home. Difficulty Level Easy Estimated Total Time ~15–30 minutes What to try first 1. Lift the tank lid and look: Is water trickling into the overflow tube (the tall pipe in the center)? If yes, your water level is too high. 2. Check the flapper chain: Is it too tight, too loose, or tangled? There should be about ½ inch of slack when the flapper is closed. 3. Do a quick dye test: Add a few drops of food coloring to the tank water, wait 15–20 minutes without flushing, then check the bowl. If color appears, the flapper is leaking. [Source: web - https://mrpipey.com/blog/is-it-bad-if-my-toilet-keeps-running] Tools you might need - Adjustable wrench or pliers (for fill valve replacement if needed) - Sponge or rag (to soak up tank water) - Bucket or towel - Food coloring (for leak testing) - Replacement flapper or fill valve kit (if adjustment doesn't work) - Rubber gloves (optional) Step-by-step fix 1. Check the water level (~2min) Remove the tank lid and look at the water level while the tank is full. It should sit about 1 inch below the top of the overflow tube. If water is flowing into the tube, the float is set too high. 2. Adjust the float (~3min) If you have a ball float (older toilets), gently bend the metal arm downward to lower the water level. If you have a cup float (newer toilets), turn the adjustment screw on the fill valve clockwise to lower the water level. Flush and check again. [Source: web - https://plumbingresource.com/fixing-a-running-toilet/] 3. Inspect and adjust the chain (~2min) Look at the chain connecting the flush handle to the flapper. It should have slight slack—about ½ inch—when the flapper is down. If it's too tight, move the clip to a lower link. If too loose, shorten it so it doesn't get stuck under the flapper. [Source: web - https://toiletfound.com/toilet-keeps-running/] 4. Test the flapper seal with dye (~15min) Add a few drops of food coloring to the tank water. Wait 15–20 minutes without flushing. If colored water appears in the bowl, the flapper is leaking and needs to be replaced or cleaned. [Source: web - https://simplehomefixesguide.com/running-toilet-even-after-replacing-the-flapper/] 5. Replace the flapper if needed (~5min) Turn off the water supply valve behind the toilet (clockwise). Flush to empty the tank. Unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube and chain, then install the new one. Reattach the chain with slight slack. Turn the water back on and test. Bring the old flapper to the store to match the size and style. [Source: web - https://www.ironmountainplumbing.com/why-your-toilet-keeps-running-and-how-to-fix-it/] 6. Replace the fill valve if it still won't shut off (~15min) If adjusting the float doesn't stop the water and you hear constant hissing, the fill valve may be worn out. Turn off the water, flush and sponge out the tank, disconnect the water supply line, unscrew the locknut under the tank, remove the old valve, and install a new one. Reconnect everything and test. [Source: web - https://plumbingresource.com/fixing-a-running-toilet/] When to call a professional - The toilet still runs after replacing the flapper and fill valve. - You see cracks in the tank, bowl, or overflow tube. - Water is leaking from the base of the toilet or onto the floor. - The shut-off valve behind the toilet is stuck, leaking, or won't fully stop the water. - You're uncomfortable working inside the tank or disconnecting the water supply line. - You have a pressure-assist or dual-flush toilet with complex internal mechanisms.
